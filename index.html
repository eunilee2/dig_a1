Dataset: Fatal Accidental Overdoses in Allegheny County 2007 - 2025

This dataset contains 7,478 entries of fatal accidental overdose cases in the Allegheny County region of Pennsylvania. It only includes cases from 2007 to 2025 that have been closed, meaning that a final conclusion about the case is made after following all steps of the procedure in administering the case investigation. Each case contains three types of information: 1. the geotemporal data of each case that covers the death date and time, case year, and incident zip, 2. demographic data that includes the case subject's age, sex, and race, and 3. the polysubstance data that covers the types of drugs used and the number of drugs used in each overdose case. One thing to note about this data is that incident zip indicates the zip code of where the Office of the Medical Examiner received the body, not necessarily where the overdose occurred. Nevertheless, as we explore the dataset, I hope to uncover more detail in understanding emerging temporal trends in drug cases and quantitative trends in the polysubstance nature of cases. Understanding this can allow us to compare overdose deaths on a year-by-year basis and potentially how current policies and interventions might play a role in these results.

This data can be found online at: https://data.wprdc.org/dataset/allegheny-county-fatal-accidental-overdoses/resource/1c59b26a-1684-4bfb-92f7-205b947530cf

Overall Analysis Questions

  1. How have fatal overdoses in Allegheny County changed over time?
  2. How do overdose patterns vary across space?
  3. Which demographic groups are the most impacted, and how do their patterns differ?
  4. What does polysubstance use look like in these overdose cases? How about single substance?

From these findings, we can then start to contextualize who is most at risk within Allegheny County. The demographic patterns suggest that fatal overdoses are not evenly distributed across the population, but rather concentrated among White and Black communities, with other races making up only a small fraction of cases. The strong male majority reflects broader national trends in substance use and overdose outcomes, highlighting potential gendered risk factors. Meanwhile, the concentration of cases among adults in their mid-20s through mid-50s underscores that prime working-age individuals bear the heaviest burden of the crisis.

Together, these observations indicate that the overdose epidemic disproportionately affects men in early-to-mid adulthood within White and Black populations. This demographic concentration raises important questions about structural inequities, access to treatment and prevention resources, and the social and economic conditions that exacerbate overdose risk.

Shifting our attention from demographics to substances, we next examine which drugs are most commonly involved in fatal overdose cases. Unsurprisingly, fentanyl dominates the dataset, appearing in nearly 3,900 cases, reflecting its well-documented role as the primary driver of the overdose crisis. Cocaine and heroin follow as the next most common substances, with alcohol also appearing frequently in combination with other drugs. Beyond these leading contributors, prescription and synthetic opioids such as alprazolam, acetyl fentanyl, para-fluorofentanyl, oxycodone, methadone, and morphine appear in smaller but still significant numbers. These findings highlight that while fentanyl remains the most lethal factor, polysubstance use (including combinations of opioids, stimulants, and alcohol) continues to play a critical role in sustaining high fatality rates.

Building on our analysis of drug types, we next compare single-drug overdose cases to those involving multiple substances. The results reveal a striking imbalance: polysubstance overdoses account for the vast majority of cases (n=5,539), while single-drug overdoses represent a much smaller portion (n=1,300). This finding emphasizes that fatal overdoses are rarely attributable to one substance alone, but more often the result of dangerous drug interactions. The prominence of polysubstance cases suggests that interventions must address not only the risks of individual drugs like fentanyl, but also the compounding dangers of combining opioids with stimulants, alcohol, or other substances.
